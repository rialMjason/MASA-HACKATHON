## 1.0 Introduction
Greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions refer to the release of gases into the Earth's atmosphere that trap heat and contribute to the greenhouse effect. These excessive emissions caused by human activities have significantly intensified global warming and climate change. The major greenhouse gases include carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), nitrous oxide (N2O), and fluorinated gases. Carbon dioxide is primarily emitted through the combustion of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas. Methane is released from agriculture, landfills, and oil and gas production. Nitrous oxide originates mainly from agricultural fertilisers and industrial processes, while fluorinated gases are synthetic gases commonly used in refrigeration, air conditioning, and electronics manufacturing.

Scientists use the Global Warming Potential (GWP) to compare the warming effects of different greenhouse gases. Carbon dioxide is assigned a GWP of 1, serving as the benchmark. Gases with higher GWP values trap more heat over a specified period, usually 100 years. This allows emissions to be measured using a common unit known as carbon dioxide equivalent (CO2e). The rapid increase in greenhouse gas concentrations since the Industrial Revolution has led to rising global temperatures, melting glaciers, rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and widespread ecological disruption.

## 2.0 United Nations Targets for Greenhouse Gas Emissions
The United Nations addresses climate change primarily through the Paris Agreement, which was adopted during the 21st Conference of the Parties (COP21) in Paris on 12 December 2015. The Paris Agreement is a legally binding international treaty under the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC).

Its primary objective is to limit the increase in global average temperature to well below 2°C above pre-industrial levels, while pursuing efforts to limit warming to 1.5°C. Scientific evidence has shown that restricting warming to 1.5°C would substantially reduce the risks of severe climate impacts, including stronger storms, prolonged droughts, sea-level rise, food insecurity, and biodiversity loss.

To achieve these goals, each participating country must submit Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs), which outline national targets, policies, and strategies for reducing greenhouse gas emissions. These commitments are reviewed and strengthened every five years under the Agreement's ratchet mechanism, ensuring that global ambition increases over time.

In addition, the United Nations has called for the world to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by around 2050. Net-zero means balancing the amount of greenhouse gases emitted with the amount removed from the atmosphere through natural carbon sinks, such as forests, or technological solutions like carbon capture and storage.

## 3.0 Southeast Asian Policies on Greenhouse Gas Emissions
Southeast Asia is among the regions most vulnerable to climate change. Its extensive coastlines, densely populated urban centres, agricultural dependence, and heavy reliance on fossil fuels make it particularly susceptible to rising sea levels, floods, droughts, and extreme weather.

### 3.1 Net zero emissions goal
Recognising these risks, many Southeast Asian countries have established long-term decarbonisation goals. Eight out of ten ASEAN member states have announced net-zero targets. Brunei, Cambodia, Laos, Malaysia, Singapore, and Vietnam aim to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050. Indonesia targets 2060, while Thailand aims for 2065.

These commitments signal the region's growing determination to align economic development with environmental sustainability. However, achieving these goals will require significant investments in renewable energy, grid modernisation, electric vehicles, industrial decarbonisation, and sustainable land management.

### 3.2 Carbon Tax in Singapore
Singapore became the first Southeast Asian country to implement a carbon tax on 1 January 2019. The tax initially applied at a rate of S$5 per tonne of carbon dioxide equivalent for large emitters.

The tax increased to S$25 per tonne in 2024 and further rose to S$45 per tonne in 2026. Singapore plans to increase the rate to between S$50 and S$80 per tonne by 2030.

Carbon taxation works by placing a direct financial cost on greenhouse gas emissions. This encourages companies to improve energy efficiency, invest in low-carbon technologies, and transition towards cleaner production methods. The policy is particularly relevant for energy-intensive industries such as petrochemicals, refining, and manufacturing.

### 3.3 Emissions Trading System in Indonesia
Indonesia launched Southeast Asia's first mandatory Emissions Trading System (ETS) in February 2023. Unlike a carbon tax, which sets a fixed price on emissions, an ETS establishes a cap on total emissions and allows companies to trade emission allowances.

Indonesia's ETS initially covers coal-fired power plants with capacities of at least 100 MW connected to the national electricity grid. Companies that emit less than their allocated allowances can sell excess permits, while those exceeding their limits must purchase additional allowances.

The Indonesian government estimates that this system could reduce greenhouse gas emissions by approximately 36 million tonnes of CO2 equivalent by 2030. The ETS represents a major milestone in Southeast Asia's transition towards market-based climate regulation.

### 3.4 Implications of the policies
Carbon pricing mechanisms such as carbon taxes and emissions trading systems are widely regarded as among the most efficient methods for reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

They create economic incentives for businesses to innovate, improve efficiency, and adopt cleaner technologies. Rather than prescribing specific technologies, these policies allow market forces to determine the most cost-effective pathways to emission reduction.

Furthermore, carbon pricing helps internalise the environmental costs of pollution, ensuring that companies bear the true social cost of their emissions.

## 4.0 Corporate Penalty Cases in Southeast Asia
### 4.1 PT Kallista Alam (Indonesia)
One of Southeast Asia's most significant environmental enforcement cases involved PT Kallista Alam, an Indonesian palm oil company.

In 2014, Indonesian courts found the company guilty of illegally burning peat swamp forests in the Tripa peatland area within the protected Leuser Ecosystem in Aceh.

This case is highly relevant to greenhouse gas emissions because peatlands are among the world's largest carbon sinks. When peatlands are drained or burned, they release massive quantities of carbon dioxide and methane into the atmosphere.

The court imposed severe penalties:
- Fine: IDR 114 billion
- Environmental restoration costs: IDR 251 billion

This landmark ruling demonstrated Indonesia's willingness to hold corporations financially accountable for activities that significantly contribute to greenhouse gas emissions and environmental degradation.

### 4.2 Carbon Tax Burden on Oil Companies in Singapore
Major multinational energy companies operating in Singapore, including ExxonMobil, Shell, and Chevron, face substantial financial obligations under Singapore's carbon tax regime.

Although these companies have not been fined for illegal conduct, the carbon tax effectively imposes a recurring cost on their greenhouse gas emissions. Given that they operate some of the region's largest oil refineries and petrochemical facilities, their annual tax liabilities are substantial.

This represents a form of regulatory pressure designed to incentivise emission reductions and cleaner operations.

## 5.0 Global Corporate Penalty Cases
### 5.1 Shell Climate Litigation (Netherlands)
In a groundbreaking 2021 decision, a Dutch court ordered Shell to reduce its global carbon dioxide emissions by 45% by 2030 compared with 2019 levels.

This was the first time a court required a multinational corporation to align its business strategy with the goals of the Paris Agreement.

### 5.2 Volkswagen Dieselgate (Germany)
The Volkswagen emissions scandal, commonly known as Dieselgate, was uncovered in 2015.

Although the scandal primarily involved nitrogen oxides rather than greenhouse gases, it remains one of the most significant emissions-related corporate scandals in history.

Volkswagen ultimately paid more than US$30 billion in fines, settlements, compensation, and vehicle buybacks.

The case illustrates the severe financial, legal, and reputational consequences that can arise from emissions-related misconduct.

## 6.0 Relationship Between Corporate Penalties and Greenhouse Gas Policies
Corporate penalties can arise through several mechanisms:
- Direct environmental violations, such as illegal burning or excessive emissions.
- Regulatory compliance costs, including carbon taxes and emissions allowance purchases.
- Climate litigation, where courts require companies to reduce emissions.
- Fraud or misrepresentation, such as falsifying emissions data.

These mechanisms play an essential role in ensuring corporate accountability and accelerating the global transition to a low-carbon economy.

## 7.0 Business Implications
Companies operating in carbon-intensive industries face several important risks:
- Financial risk from carbon taxes, fines, and compliance costs.
- Legal risk from lawsuits and regulatory enforcement.
- Reputational risk from public scrutiny and investor concerns.
- Transition risk from technological disruption and changing market preferences.

Businesses that proactively reduce emissions are better positioned to attract investors, secure sustainable financing, comply with evolving regulations, and maintain long-term competitiveness.

## 8.0 Conclusion
Greenhouse gas emissions are the primary driver of modern climate change. The United Nations, through the Paris Agreement, has established a comprehensive global framework aimed at limiting warming to 1.5°C and achieving net-zero emissions by mid-century.

Southeast Asian countries are increasingly embracing climate action through ambitious net-zero commitments, carbon taxes, and emissions trading systems. Singapore and Indonesia have emerged as regional leaders in carbon pricing and market-based climate regulation.

Corporate cases such as PT Kallista Alam demonstrate that environmental violations contributing to greenhouse gas emissions can result in substantial financial penalties. Meanwhile, global cases involving Shell and Volkswagen highlight the growing legal and regulatory expectations placed on corporations worldwide.

As climate policies continue to strengthen, effective greenhouse gas management has become a critical component of corporate governance, risk management, and long-term business sustainability. Companies that fail to adapt will likely face increasing financial, legal, and reputational consequences in the years ahead.
